Live Music Databases

Value of concert recordings

Music research

  • Corpus for music information retrieval tasks like genre detection, key detection, cover detection, etc. (Bechhofer et al. 2013)
  • Multiple recordings of same songs by same artists allow for comparison across performances (Bechhofer et al. 2013)

Archival perspective

  • Evidentiary value of material outside of a band's "official" releases
  • Different recordings of the same event can add to the collective memory by capturing multiple perspectives
  • By allowing members of the community to download and duplicate materials, online archives ensure the preservation of their materials in a way that would not be possible for traditional archives with more limitations on access (Wallace 2009)

History of tape-trading

Grateful Dead community

  • Fans taping and trading tapes of Grateful Dead concerts since the 1960s (Jones 2005)
    • Taping officially permitted by band and encouraged by special tapers’ section at concerts
    • Fans must follow rules for noncommercial use of tapes
    • Tapes traded in person and through postal system (Wallace 2009)
  • Grateful Dead tape-trading community was early adopter of networked computing and most active discussion group on the Whole Earth ‘Lectronic Link (WELL), a virtual community created in the mid-1980s (Jones 2005)
    • Networked computing made it easier for fans to seek out information on tapes and for traders to distribute their tapes
    • pre-WELL: competitive trader hierarchies, post-WELL: tapes more accessible to everyone via online trading fora (Wallace 2009)
  • Grateful Dead tape-trading community’s adoption of networked computing served as a model for later bands (e.g. Phish) to build online communities (Jones 2005)

Metadata standards

  • As tape-trading networks expanded, participants developed standards of practice with regard to documentation
    • Traders’ holding lists would generally include the date, location, venue, and a sound quality grade of the taped shows
    • Tapers’ descriptions would include recording steps and technical specifications (Wallace 2009; Jones 2005)
  • Crowdsourced efforts to compile concert and setlist data
    • DeadBase started in 1987 as one fan’s computer science masters program project and eventually became a collaborative effort between fans, with official approval and assistance from the band, to accurately document and publish the Grateful Dead’s complete concert date, venue, and setlist data (Wallace 2009)
    • Description related to any given performance may be continually improved or enriched by the community (Wallace 2009)

Examples of online collections

SugarMegs

 
  • Founded by Peter Hedeman in 1996, initially to allow browsing and download access to collection of Grateful Dead shows (SugarMegs history)
  • As of 19 January 2020, streaming and download access to recordings of 111,089 shows from 21,060 bands
  • Audio files only available in lossily compressed formats
    • Download as WMA or MP3
    • No track splitting
  • Accompanying HTML files with event information, recording/transfer information, setlists, images from shows

etree.org

  • Created in 1998 with founding principles of “large scale electronic distribution,“high quality,” and “trade-friendly only”
    • “Etree is a forum for the true audiophile, for whom perfect digital copies are a must.  Sugarmegs serves those who are more casual collectors, or who don't have access to the computer resources necessary to collect and trade shn files.” (SugarMegs history)
  • Database of metadata (db.etree.org), but audio files shared via BitTorrent (bt.etree.org)
  • Not every show listed will have source (may just have setlists), some sources will now be links to show in Live Music Archive
    • Phish shows from 2011
    • Sources for 11 June 2011 Phish show
    • BT side
    • DB side
  • Standards and guidelines for audio file formats and metadata
    • Lossless formats used to ensure high sound quality and protect files against degradation (Jones 2005)
    • Metadata entered in accompanying plaintext info files
      • Recording and transfer process information
      • Event information such as venue, date, setlist
      • Additional notes

Live Music Archive (LMA)

What is and isn't allowed in the collection

  • LMA List of Trade-Friendly Bands

Format

  • Like etree.org, files must be uploaded in lossless audio formats
    • Lossily compressed files automatically created on LMA side to ensure uniformity in quality
  • Metadata added by uploader in free-text fields
  • User reviews - content varies from comments on recording quality to memories of the show

Band-specific collections

  • Smaller fan-driven efforts
  • Hipbase.com: Tragically Hip performances stored in shared folders on Google Drive
  • Phish Spreadsheet: Google Sheets with basic concert metadata plus links to audio files hosted on MediaFire, setlists on phish.net

Projects using LMA data

  • Live Music Archive Linked Data (Bechhofer et al. 2013)
    • Motivation: Research activities would benefit from structured metadata for recordings in LMA, but existing LMA metadata inconsistent in detail and content
    • Layered approach to create RDF-structured metadata for recordings
      • Core metadata published “as is,” with raw LMA data translated to RDF with vocabulary terms from existing ontologies
      • Additional information added from mapping to other collections (e.g. MusicBrainz, last.fm, GeoNames)
      • Example RDF record for Grateful Dead
    • Created web application to allow query and browsing of collection with SPARQL
  • Computational Analysis of the Live Music Archive (CALMA) project (Bechhofer et al. 2014)
    • Extension of LMA Linked Data project
    • Extracting audio features from LMA recordings and using to perform tasks such as:
      • Identifying mislabelled songs to resolve metadata issues
      • Analyzing changes in performance of same song by same artist over time
  • Grateful Live (Wilmering et al. 2016) & Concert Stitch (Subramanian and Lerch 2018)
    • Aligning and combining different recordings of same event
  • Grateful Dead Concert Explorer (Thalmann et al. 2018)
    • Web application bringing together information from various sources to put concert recordings into context
    • Example: Laguna Hills, California - Thursday, April 27, 1989
